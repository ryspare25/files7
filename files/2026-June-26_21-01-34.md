Subtraction Adds to Your Life (Via Negativa)

Now I speculate the following, having looked closely at data with my friend Spyros Makridakis, a statistician and decision scientist…
We estimated that cutting medical expenditures by a certain amount (while limiting the cuts to elective surgeries and treatments) would extend people’s lives in most rich countries, especially the United States.
[The claim here is deeply counterintuitive but statistically grounded: reducing low-benefit medical interventions may increase overall health outcomes. This works by avoiding unnecessary iatrogenics and letting the body's natural resilience take over.]

Why? Simple basic convexity analysis; a simple examination of conditional iatrogenics: the error of treating the mildly ill puts them in a concave position.
[Treating mildly ill people who would likely recover naturally adds risk without proportional benefit. The risk/reward curve is concave—the harm grows faster than the benefit. In contrast, treating severely ill people may produce a convex outcome—potential for large improvement.]
