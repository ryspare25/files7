# Project Management Notes: Customer Relations, Communication, and Leadership

## Weekly/Monthly Reward Activities

### Surface Meaning
Give rewards regularly.

### What It Really Means

People do what gets rewarded.

A PM cannot rely solely on motivation, loyalty, professionalism, or company values. Humans naturally respond to incentives.

If team members receive recognition, bonuses, promotions, praise, certificates, or other rewards for good performance, they are more likely to repeat that behavior.

The key lesson is:

> Desired behavior must be reinforced repeatedly.

If rewards are only given during annual evaluations, people lose the connection between effort and reward.

- Weekly rewards influence short-term behavior.
- Monthly rewards influence medium-term behavior.
- Annual rewards influence long-term retention.

A good PM understands:

> People do not simply manage projects. They manage incentive systems.

## Maintain a Win-Win Spirit with Customers

### Surface Meaning
Be cooperative with customers.

### Deeper Meaning

The customer is not your enemy.

Many failed projects become adversarial:

- Customers try to squeeze additional work.
- Vendors try to minimize effort.
- Customers hide concerns.
- Vendors hide problems.

Eventually, everyone spends more energy protecting themselves than solving project issues.

A win-win mentality asks:

Instead of:

> How do I protect my side?

Ask:

> How do we both succeed?

### Example

Bad PM:

> That's outside scope. Not my problem.

Good PM:

> That's outside scope, but let's discuss possible solutions.

The project becomes a partnership instead of a battlefield.

## Don't Apologize If You Don't Know the Mistake — Or You'll Repeat It

### Common Misinterpretation

This does **not** mean:

> Never apologize.

It means:

> Never apologize before understanding the root cause.

### What Inexperienced PMs Often Do

Customer:

> Why did this happen?

PM:

> Sorry, sorry, sorry.

Customer:

> What caused it?

PM:

> I'm not sure yet.

The apology becomes meaningless because there is no understanding behind it.

### Stronger Approach

Instead say:

> We acknowledge the issue. We are investigating the root cause and will provide findings and corrective actions.

After investigation:

> We identified the cause. Here is what happened, why it happened, and how we will prevent recurrence.

### Why This Matters

Unknown mistake = unknown prevention.

If you don't know why something happened, you cannot stop it from happening again.

Real professionalism follows this sequence:

1. Understand
2. Fix
3. Prevent
4. Apologize when appropriate

Not:

1. Apologize
2. Panic
3. Guess
4. Repeat

## Customers Are Not Always Right and Don't Always Know the Real Project Status

### Why?

Customers typically see:

- Deliverables
- Meetings
- Reports
- Demonstrations

The PM sees:

- Risks
- Technical debt
- Resource shortages
- Internal conflicts
- Hidden blockers
- Schedule pressure

The customer's understanding is limited by the information available to them.

### Example

Customer:

> The project is 90% complete.

Development team:

> Integration testing hasn't started yet.

Customer:

> Then it's not really 90% complete.

Exactly.

Customers often estimate progress based on visible output.

PMs must estimate progress based on actual remaining work.

Sometimes a PM must respectfully state:

> I understand your perspective, but the actual project status differs from what appears on the surface.

A PM's responsibility is truth, not agreement.

## PM Must Provide Full, Regular Status Updates

### Why?

Projects become dangerous when information stops flowing.

When information is missing:

- Customers imagine worst-case scenarios.
- Management creates assumptions.
- Team members start rumors.

A good PM reduces uncertainty.

### Every Status Report Should Answer

#### Where are we?
Current status.

#### What was completed?
Achievements since the last report.

#### What remains?
Remaining work.

#### What risks exist?
Potential threats.

#### What help is needed?
Escalations and decisions.

The goal is not paperwork.

The goal is trust.

> People trust projects that are visible.

> People distrust projects that are silent.

## Discuss Customer Issues Frankly but Tactfully So They Can Improve

This is a political and communication skill.

Many PMs choose one of two extremes.

### Extreme 1: Brutal Honesty

> Your requirements are bad.

> Your team caused delays.

> Your process is inefficient.

Result:

The customer becomes defensive.

### Extreme 2: Avoiding Conflict

> Everything is fine.

> No concerns.

> No issues.

Result:

Problems never get fixed.

### The Skilled PM's Approach

Honest + Diplomatic.

Instead of:

> Your requirements are terrible.

Say:

> We noticed several requirement changes during development. Establishing a more stable review process may help reduce rework and improve schedule predictability.

The message remains the same.

The delivery changes.

The objective is improvement, not humiliation.

A PM must sometimes communicate uncomfortable truths.

The customer should leave thinking:

> They helped us identify the problem.

Not:

> They attacked us.

# The Underlying PM Philosophy

All six points reduce to one principle:

> A Project Manager is primarily an information manager and relationship manager.

The PM's job is not simply schedules, budgets, Gantt charts, or status reports.

The PM's real responsibilities are:

- Align incentives (rewards)
- Preserve partnerships (win-win thinking)
- Find causes instead of assigning blame (root cause analysis)
- Communicate reality (status reporting)
- Correct misunderstandings (customers are not always right)
- Deliver difficult truths diplomatically

### PM Maturity Levels

**Weak PM**
- Manages tasks.

**Strong PM**
- Manages expectations.

**Great PM**
- Manages expectations before they become problems.
