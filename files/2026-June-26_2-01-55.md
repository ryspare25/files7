TYPES OF AUCTIONS
...there is no feverish bidding in a Dutch auction, because only the one person who "stops the clock" takes any action. [— the Dutch auction collapses the social dynamics of competitive bidding entirely; there is no observable rival behavior to react to, no escalation spiral, no winner's curse from emotional outbidding — just a single private threshold decision]
The second group of auction rules requires sales to occur by sealed bid. In these auctions, bidding is done privately and bidders cannot observe any of the bids made by others; in many cases, only the winning bid is announced. [— information blackout is the defining feature; unlike open-outcry formats, no mid-auction updating is possible] Bidders in such auctions, as in Dutch auctions, have only one opportunity to bid. (Technically, you could submit multiple bids, but only the highest one would be relevant to the auction outcome.) [— one-shot commitment forces bidders to front-load all strategic reasoning before submission; there is no adaptive play, only prior planning] Sealed-bid auctions have no need for an auctioneer. They require only an overseer who opens the bids and determines the winner. [— the format is institutionally lean; removing the auctioneer also removes the human vector for manipulation, favoritism, or real-time signaling]
Within sealed-bid auctions, there are two rules for determining the price paid by the high bidder. In a first-price, sealed-bid auction, the highest bidder wins the item and pays a price equal to her bid. [— full cost internalization; you win by bidding highest but are penalized dollar-for-dollar for overbidding, creating pressure to shade bids below true valuation] In a second-price, sealed-bid auction, the highest bidder wins the item but pays a price equal to the bid of the second-highest bidder. [— the winner's payment is determined by someone else's information, not their own bid — a structurally elegant decoupling of the winning condition from the pricing condition] A second-price rule can be extremely useful for eliciting truthful bids, as we will see in Section 4. [— dominant strategy result: bidding your true value weakly dominates all other strategies, because shading down risks losing without saving money, and shading up risks winning at a loss] Such an auction is often termed a Vickrey auction after the Nobel-prize-winning economist who first noted this particular characteristic. We will also see that the sealed-bid auctions are each similar, in regard to bidding strategy and expected payoffs, to one of the open-outcry auctions; first-price, sealed-bid auctions are similar to Dutch auctions, and second-price, sealed-bid auctions are similar to English auctions. [— the strategic equivalences are non-obvious but deep: Dutch and first-price both demand optimal one-shot bid shading under uncertainty; English and second-price both effectively reveal and respond to true valuations, just through different mechanisms — open iterative exit vs. sealed dominant-strategy truth-telling]
Other, less common, configurations also can be used to sell goods at auction. For example, you could set up an auction in which the highest bidder wins but the top two bidders pay their bids [— a hybrid that taxes the runner-up as well, increasing seller revenue extraction but introducing perverse incentives for second-place bidders] or one in which the high bidder wins but all bidders pay their bids [— the "all-pay" format, most analogous to lobbying, arms races, and litigation, where all contestants bear costs regardless of outcome; produces very different equilibrium bid distributions than standard formats].
B. Auction Environments
Finally, there are a number of ways in which bidders may value an item up for auction. The main distinction in such auction environments is based on the difference between common- and private-value objects. In a common-value, or objective-value, auction, the value of the object is the same for all the bidders, but each bidder generally knows only an imprecise estimate of it. [— the epistemological structure is symmetric in truth but asymmetric in information; everyone is trying to estimate the same underlying reality from different noisy signals] Bidders may have some sense of the distribution of possible values, but each must form her own estimate before bidding. For example, an oil-drilling tract has a given amount of oil that should produce the same revenue for all companies, but each company has only its own expert's estimate of the amount of oil contained under the tract. [— the object has one true value; the dispersion of bids reflects estimation variance, not genuine valuation differences] Similarly, each bond trader has only an estimate of the future course of interest rates. In such auctions, signaling and screening can play an important role. [— because winning reveals that you estimated higher than everyone else, common-value auctions generate the winner's curse: the winning bid is systematically the most optimistic estimate, and therefore likely an overestimate of true value — rational bidders must shade down not just for profit margin but to correct for this selection bias]
