I can explain the argument, but not in a propaganda or celebratory voice.
This passage is making a centralization argument: it says the military should not preserve local identities—Bavarian, Baden, Württemberg, Prussian, etc.—but should deliberately turn recruits into people who identify first with one unified German nation.
In plain language:
“Don’t keep soldiers in their home regions.”
The author thinks a Bavarian training in Munich stays mentally “just Bavarian,” while someone from Hamburg stays “just northern German.” He wants recruits moved around the country.
Use the army as a national identity factory.
A recruit from Bavaria should see the Rhine and North Sea; a recruit from Hamburg should see the Alps; someone from East Prussia should see central Germany. The intended result is emotional: “this whole map is mine; I belong to all of it.”
Local culture may remain cosmetic, but loyalty must be centralized.
The passage allows regional “character” in troops, but rejects regional autonomy in training, command structure, and political identity. In effect: keep the accents and folk costumes if you want, but erase competing loyalties.
Federalism is treated as an obstacle.
The older German system had powerful regional states with their own traditions and institutions. The author frames those borders as chains holding back the nation. The desired state is not a federation of regions; it is a single ideological nation-state.
The army is only one piece of a larger project.
The closing lines reveal the bigger purpose: National Socialism claims the right to impose its principles across all German territory, regardless of existing state boundaries. The comparison to churches is strategic: churches do not stop their mission at provincial borders, so the movement says its political mission should not either.
The dangerous core is that this is not merely “national unity is good.” It turns national unity into a justification for subordinating regional pluralism, local self-rule, and eventually individual independence to one state ideology. The military becomes a tool for social and political remolding—not simply defense.
A concise modern paraphrase would be:
“Stop treating Germany as a patchwork of regional identities. Use military service to mix young men across the country, detach them from local loyalties, and train them to see the nation—and the ideology governing it—as their primary identity. Then extend that central authority over every former regional boundary.”
