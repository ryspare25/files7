wolfofwallst A million people on that beach. Every one of them complaining about the same thing. The walk to the concession stand was too long.

I was sixteen, and I heard something nobody else did. A million people telling me exactly what they wanted and exactly what was standing in their way. Free market research, and I was the only one listening.

The next morning I spent twenty two dollars. A cooler, a bag of ice, and ice cream. By the afternoon it was empty. The next day I brought two. Then four. By the end of that summer I had earned more than any kid on that sand made all year.

The product was never ice cream. It was convenience. Everybody out there felt the same heat and stood in the same line, and not one of them did a thing about it. I was just the only one willing to move.

Opportunities almost never show up looking like opportunities. They show up looking like problems. The complaint you keep hearing is the market telling you what it needs. Everybody gets their version of that beach. Most people go back to their towel. A few buy the cooler.

Summer of 1978. Jones Beach. The summer I found out I loved sales.
