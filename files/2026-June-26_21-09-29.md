Decision-making is only one of the tasks of an executive [i.e., a person who carries ultimate responsibility for the direction, health, and results of an organization].
It usually takes but a small fraction of his time [often a few focused moments amid many operational chores].
But to make decisions is the specific executive task [the unique contribution no one else in the enterprise can legitimately perform].

Decision-making therefore deserves special treatment in a discussion of the effective executive [because its quality can make or break overall performance].
Only executives make decisions [as opposed to technicians who recommend or analyze].
Indeed, to be expected—by virtue of position or knowledge—to make decisions that have significant impact on the entire organization, its performance, and results defines the executive [this accountability is the job’s core identity]. (mission, shimei)
